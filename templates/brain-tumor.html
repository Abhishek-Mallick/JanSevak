Brain Tumor Detection

A brain tumor is an abnormal growth of cells in the brain. It can be either cancerous (malignant) or non-cancerous (benign). Brain tumors can arise from the brain itself (primary tumors) or spread to the brain from other parts of the body (secondary tumors or metastatic tumors).

The treatment of brain tumors depends on several factors, including the type, location, size, and grade of the tumor, as well as the overall health of the patient. The main treatment options for brain tumors include:

â­• Surgery: Surgical removal of the tumor is often the first-line treatment when possible. The surgeon aims to remove as much of the tumor as safely possible while preserving brain function.
â­• Radiation Therapy: Radiation therapy uses high-energy beams to kill or shrink tumor cells. It may be used before or after surgery or as the primary treatment for inoperable tumors.
â­• Chemotherapy: Chemotherapy involves the use of drugs to kill cancer cells. It can be administered orally or intravenously and may be used alone or in combination with surgery and radiation therapy.
â­• Targeted Therapy: Targeted therapy uses drugs that specifically target certain molecules or pathways involved in the growth and survival of cancer cells. These therapies can be effective in treating certain types of brain tumors.
â­• Immunotherapy: Immunotherapy works by stimulating the body's immune system to recognize and attack cancer cells. It is a rapidly evolving field, and some immunotherapies have shown promising results in treating certain types of brain tumors.
â­• Supportive Care: In addition to the specific treatments, supportive care is important to manage symptoms, provide pain relief, and improve the quality of life of patients with brain tumors. This may include medications, physical therapy, occupational therapy, and psychological support.
